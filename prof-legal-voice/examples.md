# Examples: professional legal voice

Every example below is fictional. The clients, firms, amounts and dates are invented for illustration and do not describe a real matter. Facts in each example are complete: nothing is left as a placeholder.

## Example 1: routine client update

### Scenario

A solicitor acts for Daniel Wren in a property settlement following separation. The parties have agreed to obtain an independent valuation of the former family home at 22 Grosvenor Street, Turramurra before finalising consent orders. The valuer, engaged jointly, has returned a valuation of $1,450,000, close to the $1,400,000 both parties had estimated. The next step is exchanging draft consent orders, targeted for 30 July 2026. Nothing is required of Mr Wren at this stage.

### Poor version

I hope this email finds you well. I am writing to provide you with an update in relation to the above matter. Please be advised that the valuation report has now been received. We will review the report in due course and revert to you with further information as soon as possible. Please do not hesitate to contact me if you have any questions.

### Improved version

The valuer has returned a report valuing the Turramurra property at $1,450,000. That is close to the $1,400,000 figure both you and your former partner estimated, so it should not change the shape of the settlement we have discussed. There is nothing you need to do at this stage. I will use the valuation to prepare draft consent orders and aim to send them to you by 30 July 2026 for your review.

### Why the improved version works

A routine update exists to tell the client what happened and whether anything is required of them, and the reader's actual need here is reassurance grounded in a number rather than a general status report. The poor version reports that a document arrived without saying what it says or what it means, so the client is left waiting for the update that matters. The improved version states the figure, compares it against the client's own expectation so he can judge it for himself, confirms no action is required now, and gives a concrete next step with a date. Every figure and date is drawn from the facts of the instructions; nothing is invented.

## Example 2: adverse tribunal result

### Scenario

A solicitor acts for Anh Tran, who applied to the Civil and Administrative Tribunal for an order requiring a builder to rectify defective waterproofing at her unit. The Tribunal has dismissed the application, finding that Ms Tran's expert report did not adequately address the cause of the water ingress. Ms Tran has 28 days from the date of the decision to file an appeal on a question of law. The decision was handed down on 14 July 2026, so the appeal deadline is 11 August 2026. A further, more detailed expert report addressing causation could support an appeal, at an estimated further cost of $4,500.

### Poor version

There has been a minor development in your matter. The Tribunal has made its decision and unfortunately it was not entirely in your favour this time. We are considering our options and will be in touch soon to discuss the way forward.

### Improved version

The Tribunal has refused your application. It found that the expert report did not sufficiently address what caused the water to get in, and that gap was fatal to the claim. This is a genuine setback: as things stand, the builder is not required to carry out any rectification work. You have a right of appeal on a question of law, and the deadline to file it is 11 August 2026. A further expert report specifically addressing causation, at an estimated cost of $4,500, could strengthen an appeal, though it would not guarantee one succeeds. I will call you on Monday to talk through whether to commission that report and file an appeal.

### Why the improved version works

Adverse news is still a professional communication with a job to do: state the result accurately, explain what it changes, and give the client a real option before the deadline closes. The poor version calls a dismissal a minor development, which is not an accurate description of an outcome that leaves the client without the rectification order she sought, and it gives no deadline and no option. The improved version states the result in plain terms, explains why the application failed, states the practical effect, gives the actual appeal deadline, and describes a specific, costed option rather than a vague promise to be in touch. Nothing here overstates the strength of a future appeal; the report is described only as something that could help, and success is never promised.

## Example 3: request for instructions on an offer

### Scenario

A solicitor acts for Coastline Joinery Pty Ltd in a dispute with a former supplier over an unpaid invoice of $118,000. The supplier's solicitors have made a without prejudice offer to settle for $95,000 inclusive of costs, to be paid within 14 days of acceptance, in exchange for a full release of all claims arising from the contract. The offer expires at 5pm on 5 August 2026. Continuing to trial would involve further costs estimated at $40,000 and carries the usual risk that costs could be ordered against Coastline Joinery if it is unsuccessful.

### Poor version

We have received an offer from the other side. We would appreciate your instructions on how you would like to proceed. Please let us know your thoughts at your earliest convenience.

### Improved version

The supplier's solicitors have offered to settle for $95,000, inclusive of costs, paid within 14 days of acceptance, in exchange for a release of all claims under the contract. The offer expires at 5pm on 5 August 2026. Accepting it would resolve the matter now for $95,000 against the $118,000 claimed, with the shortfall representing the cost of certainty. Rejecting it and continuing to trial would involve a further estimated $40,000 in costs, and carries the usual risk that Coastline Joinery could be ordered to pay the supplier's costs if the claim is unsuccessful at trial. We need your instructions on whether to accept the offer, reject it, or make a counter-offer, by 1 August 2026 so there is time to respond before the deadline.

### Why the improved version works

A request for instructions exists to put a decision in the client's hands, so the reader's need is the complete terms of the offer and the practical consequence of each choice, well beyond a bare notice that an offer exists. The poor version asks for instructions without giving the client anything to decide between. The improved version sets out the offer's terms, states plainly what accepting and rejecting each mean in dollar and risk terms, and gives a deadline for the client's decision that leaves room to act before the offer itself expires.

## Example 4: costs update separating incurred costs and estimate

### Scenario

A solicitor acts for Renata Oyelaran in an unfair dismissal claim before the Fair Work Commission. Costs incurred to date are $18,200 including GST, charged on the firm's standard hourly rates. The estimate to take the matter through to a arbitrated hearing is a further $32,000. Under the general costs rule that applies to unfair dismissal proceedings, each party ordinarily bears its own costs, and an order that a party pay the other side's costs is only available in limited circumstances, such as an unreasonable act or omission by that party.

### Poor version

Please find below a summary of costs in your matter. Costs to date are approximately $18,200. Costs going forward will depend on how the matter proceeds, but should not be excessive. There is generally limited costs risk in this jurisdiction, so this should not be a major concern for you.

### Improved version

Costs incurred on your matter to date total $18,200, including GST, charged at the firm's standard hourly rates. Taking the matter through to an arbitrated hearing is estimated to cost a further $32,000. In unfair dismissal proceedings before the Fair Work Commission, each side generally bears its own costs, and an order that you pay the other party's costs is only available in limited circumstances, such as an unreasonable act or omission on your part during the proceedings. On the facts as they stand, we do not see a basis on which such an order would be made against you, but the general costs risk in this jurisdiction is not zero. Please let us know if you would like to discuss whether to continue to hearing given the further cost estimated.

### Why the improved version works

A costs update exists so the client can make a financial decision with real numbers, and the reader's need is three distinct figures: what has already been spent, what is still to come, and what could go wrong. The poor version blends incurred costs and a future estimate into a single vague reassurance and tells the client not to worry, which minimises a genuine, if limited, financial risk. The improved version keeps the incurred figure, the estimate, and the costs-order risk separate, states the applicable costs rule accurately without overstating how protective it is, and invites the client to make the decision the update exists to support.
