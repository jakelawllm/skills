# Register guide: professional legal voice

This guide sets out short patterns for the client communications this skill covers. Use the pattern that matches the situation rather than one template for every message. A routine update reads very differently from an adverse outcome, and a costs update needs its own structure because the client's decision depends on getting the numbers right.

## Routine client update

1. State the development.
2. Say what it means for the client, including whether it is expected or routine.
3. State whether anything is required of the client now, or that nothing is required.
4. State the next step and who is responsible for it.

Keep a routine update short. If nothing has changed and no decision is needed, say so plainly rather than padding the message to look substantial.

## Advice with risk

1. State the answer or the position first.
2. Explain what it means for the client in practical terms.
3. State the risk, the uncertainty, or the limitation honestly.
4. Explain the realistic options available.
5. Say who will do what, and by when.
6. Ask for instructions if a decision is required.

A risk stated once, plainly, does more for the client than a risk buried in qualifications. Do not let reassurance crowd out the risk. State the risk before any reassurance so the client actually reads it.

## Adverse outcome

1. State the result plainly, without a softening label.
2. Explain its practical effect on the client's position.
3. Identify any immediate deadline or exposure.
4. Explain the realistic options, including the option of doing nothing if that is genuinely available.
5. State the next contact or action, with a date.

Do not use a euphemism such as "a minor development" for a result that is a material setback. State the result, then move directly to what the client can do about it.

## Request for instructions

1. State the matter requiring a decision, such as an offer received.
2. Set out its material terms accurately and completely.
3. State the practical consequence of accepting, and of not accepting, in concrete terms.
4. State the deadline for a decision.
5. State what the solicitor recommends, if instructed to give a recommendation, kept separate from the neutral statement of terms.

Give the client the terms before the recommendation, so the client can see what the offer actually contains rather than only the solicitor's view of it.

## Costs update

1. State the costs incurred to date, as an exact figure.
2. State the basis on which those costs were charged.
3. State the estimate of costs to complete the current stage, separately from costs incurred.
4. State any possible future exposure, such as an adverse costs order, separately again.
5. State the decision the client needs to make, if any.

Keep incurred costs, the estimate, and future exposure in three distinct figures. Do not blend them into a single reassuring total, and do not use language that minimises a genuine financial risk.

## Delay or adjournment

1. State that the matter has been delayed or adjourned, and the new date if one is set.
2. State the reason, to the extent it is known and can properly be shared.
3. State the practical effect on the client, including any cost or timing consequence.
4. State whether anything is required of the client because of the delay.

A delay is not automatically bad news, but it is not automatically nothing either. State the reason and the effect rather than assuming the client will read a bare notice of adjournment as reassuring.

## Settlement offer explanation

1. State that an offer has been made or received, and by whom.
2. Set out its material terms.
3. Explain what accepting it would mean in practical terms, including any amount, timing and release of claims.
4. Explain what rejecting it or making a counter-offer would mean, including cost and risk consequences of continuing.
5. State the deadline for a response and what the client needs to decide.

Keep the explanation of terms separate from any recommendation, so the client's decision is visibly based on the terms rather than on the solicitor's opinion alone.

## Closing a matter

1. State that the matter is now concluded and the outcome reached.
2. Confirm any final steps taken, such as registration, payment, or filing of a notice of discontinuance.
3. State the final costs position.
4. State what the client should keep, such as originals of documents or a settlement deed, and for how long.
5. State whether any ongoing obligation survives closure, such as a confidentiality term, and confirm there is nothing further required unless that is not true.
