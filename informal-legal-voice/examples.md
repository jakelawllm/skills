# Examples: informal legal voice

Every example below is fictional. The clients, firms, amounts and dates are invented for illustration and do not describe a real matter. Facts in each example are complete: nothing is left as a placeholder.

## Example 1: short routine update

### Scenario

A solicitor has an easy, established relationship with a client, Marcus Diallo, who is buying a townhouse at 7 Bellevue Lane, Newtown. The building inspection report has come back clear, with no defects noted. Exchange of contracts is set for 22 July 2026, and nothing further is needed from Marcus before then.

### Poor version

I hope you're doing well! Just a quick note to let you know that we have received the building inspection report. We will be reviewing this in due course and will be in touch with any further updates. Please don't hesitate to reach out if you have any questions in the meantime!

### Improved version

Good news, the building report came back clean, no defects. We're on track to exchange on 22 July. Nothing you need to do before then.

### Why the improved version works

A routine update to an established client only needs to say what happened and whether anything is required, and the plain register lets it do that in three short sentences instead of a padded paragraph. The poor version spends its length on stock openers and closers and never actually says what the report found. The improved version gives the one fact that matters, the good outcome, and confirms there is nothing to do, which is what a quick, informal check-in is for.

## Example 2: request for a missing document

### Scenario

A solicitor acting for an established client, Priya Sharma, in a small business loan matter needs Priya's most recent business activity statement to finish preparing the loan application before the bank's internal deadline of 25 July 2026.

### Poor version

Just following up on a few outstanding items for your file. When you get a chance, could you send through whatever paperwork you have relating to your business? Thanks so much!

### Improved version

Quick one, I need your most recent business activity statement to finish the loan application. Can you send it through by Friday 24 July so I've got a day up my sleeve before the bank's deadline on the 25th?

### Why the improved version works

A document request works when the reader knows exactly what to find and by when, and vague phrasing such as "whatever paperwork you have" forces the client to guess what is actually needed. The improved version names the specific document, states why the date matters, and gives a concrete deadline a day ahead of the real one, which is the kind of practical detail an established, informal relationship can carry without sounding stiff.

## Example 3: plain explanation of a procedural step

### Scenario

A solicitor needs to explain to an established client, Tom Halvorsen, why his small claims matter in the Local Court has been listed for a mention before it is listed for hearing.

### Poor version

Please be advised that your matter has been listed for a mention. A mention is a procedural step in the litigation process that occurs prior to the substantive hearing of the matter, at which the court will typically make directions.

### Improved version

Your case has a mention date first, 4 August 2026, before the actual hearing. A mention is just a short court date where the magistrate checks how things are progressing and sets a timetable, nothing gets decided about who wins on that day. Your actual hearing will be listed after that.

### Why the improved version works

A plain explanation needs to translate a procedural term into something the client can picture, and jargon such as "prior to the substantive hearing of the matter" defeats that purpose even though it is accurate. The improved version keeps the same accurate content, a mention comes before the hearing and sets a timetable, but says it the way you would say it out loud to someone unfamiliar with courts, and it is explicit that nothing about the outcome happens at that date.

## Example 4: adverse news expressed plainly

### Scenario

A solicitor acting for an established client, Grace Kelleher, has just received the Tribunal's decision refusing her application for a review of a Centrelink debt decision. The Tribunal found Grace had not provided enough evidence of her income during the relevant period. Grace can ask for the debt to be paused while she gathers further evidence and lodges an appeal, and the appeal deadline is 21 days from the decision date of 15 July 2026, meaning 5 August 2026.

News this serious would ordinarily route to prof-legal-voice under router item 5. This example is the hard case: an established client has asked for plain messages, so it shows the floor of accuracy the informal register must hold to when it does carry adverse news.

### Poor version

Small hiccup with the Tribunal outcome, but honestly nothing to stress about. These things happen all the time and we'll sort it out.

### Improved version

The Tribunal refused your application. It found there wasn't enough evidence of your income for that period. I know that's not the outcome you wanted. The debt itself doesn't have to be paid straight away, we can ask for it to be paused while we get more evidence together and lodge an appeal. The appeal deadline is 5 August. I'll call you tomorrow to go through what evidence we still need.

### Why the improved version works

Adverse news still needs to be accurate even in the softest register, and calling a lost review a small hiccup misdescribes an outcome that leaves a real debt in place and a short appeal window running. The improved version states the result plainly, gives the reason the Tribunal gave, and moves straight to what can actually be done, pausing the debt and lodging an appeal by a stated date, without pretending the outcome was minor. The tone stays gentle and personal, which this register is for, but nothing about the substance is softened to make it easier to deliver.
