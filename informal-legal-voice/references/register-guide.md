# Register guide: informal legal voice

This guide sets out short patterns for the messages this skill covers. It is a narrower set than the professional register guide, because this skill is for routine, low-stakes, or expressly informal communication with an established client. Serious or ambiguous work belongs in the professional register instead.

Serious content can be plain without becoming casual. A message can be short, warm and easy to read while still stating a real risk or a genuine setback accurately. Informality is a choice about sentence length, contractions and warmth. It is never a choice to soften or blur a legal fact.

## Routine update

State what happened, say whether anything is needed from the client, and say what happens next. Two or three short sentences are usually enough. Do not pad a routine update with pleasantries to make it feel more substantial.

## Document request

Say exactly what document or piece of information is needed, why it is needed if that helps the client find it, and by when. A specific request gets a faster answer than a general one, so name the document rather than asking for "a few things" or "some paperwork".

## Short explanation

State the procedural step plainly, then say what it means for the client in one plain sentence. Do not explain a step at length if a sentence will do, and do not leave a legal term unexplained just because the message is short.

## Appointment confirmation

State the date, time, place or call method, and what the client should bring or prepare, if anything. Confirm rather than merely propose, if the time is already agreed.

## Minor delay

State that something will take a little longer than expected, give the reason if it is known, and give the new expected timing. A genuine minor delay can be described as exactly that, a minor delay, because it is true. Do not use the same soft language for something that is not actually minor.

## Positive development

State the good news plainly and say what it means practically, without overselling it. A good outcome does not need extra adjectives to land; the fact itself carries the weight.

## Adverse development that still requires a professional tone

State the result plainly and accurately, the way you would if the client were sitting across the desk. Say what it means and what happens next. Warmth here means being gentle and direct. Vagueness and breeziness are not warmth, whatever they might sound like. Do not describe a real setback as a hiccup, and do not say there is nothing to worry about unless that is literally true. A plain, accurate sentence such as "the Tribunal refused the application" is warmer, in the way that matters, than a soft phrase that leaves the client unsure what actually happened.

## SMS-length message

Say the one thing the client needs to know in a single short message, and nothing else. If a decision or a risk needs explaining, that explanation does not belong in a text message: send the short message to flag it, and follow up by phone or email with the fuller explanation.
