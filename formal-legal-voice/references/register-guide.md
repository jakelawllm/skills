# Register guide: formal legal voice

This guide sets out short patterns for the document types this skill covers. Use the pattern that matches the document rather than a single template for every formal document. A letter to the other side, a file note and a submission each need their own structure.

## What kind of document is it

Formal legal documents fall into four functions. Identify the function before drafting, because the function decides the register.

A persuasive document argues a position to a reader who can be moved: a letter to the other side, written submissions, a demand. Take a position, support it, and press it.

An analytical document works out an answer for a reader who needs a candid view: an advice, a memorandum. Set out both sides of a question honestly, including the point against the client's own position, before reaching a conclusion.

An administrative document manages process for a reader who needs procedural information rather than persuasion: a letter to the court or registry about listing, filing or timetabling.

An evidentiary or record document captures what happened for a reader who will rely on the record later, including the file owner and, potentially, a costs assessor or a court: a file note, a chronology introduction. Write it neutrally. It is not the place to argue the client's case.

Confusing these functions is the most common failure in formal drafting. An advice written to persuade instead of to advise omits the counterargument the client most needs. A file note written to advocate is a poor record because it cannot be trusted as neutral.

## Letter to opponent

This is a persuasive document. Use this order where it fits the letter.

1. State the immediate issue or position in the first line.
2. State the material fact or contention.
3. Give the reason, the source, or the contractual basis for the position.
4. State the consequence that follows.
5. Make the request, response, or proposal, with a date if one is required.

Report the other side's position in controlled indirect speech: "your client contends", "we do not accept", "it is not in dispute that". Do not editorialise about the other side's conduct or motives beyond what the facts support. Precision creates firmness; adjectives do very little.

## Letter to court or registry

This document is administrative rather than persuasive. State the proceeding, the specific procedural request, the rule or direction it is made under if relevant, and the outcome sought, in plain sequence. Do not argue the merits of the substantive dispute in a listing or filing letter; confine it to the procedural matter raised. Use a respectful, formal register throughout: the reader is the court itself or its registry staff, and the letter is not addressed to an opposing party.

## Written submissions

This is a persuasive but analytical document: it argues a position, and the argument must be built from fact, authority and reasoning rather than assertion. Use this order where it fits.

1. Proposition: state the point the submission establishes.
2. Material facts or evidence supporting the proposition, each traceable to the record.
3. The applicable authority or rule.
4. Application of the authority or rule to the facts.
5. Conclusion or the relief sought.

Keep the analytical link between fact, law and conclusion. A submission that states a fact and moves on without connecting it to the authority and the conclusion asks the reader to do the reasoning that counsel is retained to do. Use the voice of counsel or a solicitor: submit, contend, accept, concede, or do not press a point. The reader decides the issue, and submitting the argument is the writer's task.

## Formal advice

This is an analytical document for a reader who needs a candid, balanced answer rather than persuasion. Use this order where it fits.

1. The question or issue asked.
2. The relevant facts and any assumption made because a fact was not supplied.
3. The applicable law or contractual provision.
4. Analysis, including the argument against the client's preferred position where one exists.
5. Risk, uncertainty and any live counterargument.
6. Conclusion.
7. Recommended action.

Do not soften a genuine risk to make the advice more comfortable to deliver. An advice that omits the counterargument the client is most likely to face in practice has failed at its one job.

## Memorandum

An internal memorandum follows the same analytical order as a formal advice: issue, facts and assumptions, law, analysis, risk and counterargument, conclusion, recommended action. The audience changes; the required rigour does not. Because the reader is another lawyer on the file, a memorandum can assume shared background and move faster through settled points, but it must still carry the analytical steps that a supervising solicitor needs to check the reasoning.

## File note

A file note is an evidentiary record. Its purpose is to preserve an accurate account of what was said and done, and advocacy has no place in it. Use a neutral structure.

1. Date and method of the communication.
2. Participants.
3. Subject matter.
4. Material information given or received.
5. Advice given.
6. Instructions received.
7. Undertakings or deadlines arising.
8. Next action and who is responsible for it.

Write what was said and done. Do not add argument, characterisation of the other side, or a persuasive gloss. A file note that reads like a letter to the other side has stopped being a reliable record, because a later reader cannot tell which parts are fact and which are advocacy.

## Chronology introduction

A chronology is an evidentiary record presented as a list of dated events, and its introduction should match that function. State the proceeding, the period covered, and the source of each entry (affidavit, exhibit, correspondence, transcript) in a short opening paragraph. Do not use the introduction to argue significance. If a submission needs to draw a conclusion from the chronology, put that conclusion in the submission and keep the chronology itself a plain list of dated events.
