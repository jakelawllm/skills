# Examples: formal legal voice

Every example below is fictional. The clients, firms, amounts and dates are invented for illustration and do not describe a real matter. Any case name is marked as fictional and must never be treated as a real authority. Facts in each example are complete: nothing is left as a placeholder.

## Example 1: letter to the other side rejecting a factual contention

### Scenario

Solicitors for Harrow Funds Management Pty Ltd, the landlord under a retail lease of shop 4, 118 Overton Road, Marrickville, write to the solicitors for the tenant, Bellcourt Cafe Pty Ltd. The tenant's solicitors have asserted that the landlord's property manager orally agreed, in a phone call on 12 January 2026, to waive the CPI rent review due on 1 July 2026. Clause 22.3 of the lease requires any waiver to be recorded in writing and signed by both parties. No signed waiver exists. The reviewed rent, calculated under clause 9.1, is $86,400 per annum, up from $81,600.

### Poor version

We refer to the above matter and to your letter dated 15 July 2026. We note the contents of your correspondence and the position taken on behalf of your client. Whilst we understand your client's view, our client's position is that the lease terms should be observed by all parties, and we would ask that your client reconsider its stance in due course so that this matter can be resolved amicably going forward.

### Improved version

Your client contends that our client's property manager orally waived the CPI rent review on 12 January 2026. We do not accept that any waiver was agreed. Clause 22.3 of the lease requires a waiver to be recorded in writing and signed by both parties, and no such document exists. Our client will accordingly invoice rent from 1 July 2026 at the reviewed rate of $86,400 per annum under clause 9.1. If your client holds a signed written waiver, please provide it by 4pm on 31 July 2026. In the absence of that document, our client will treat the reviewed rent as payable from 1 July 2026.

### Why the improved version works

This is a persuasive document addressed to an opponent who can be moved by a well supported position, so the letter needs to do more than record a disagreement. The poor version never states what is actually contended, never identifies the contractual clause that answers it, and never makes a concrete request, so the reader cannot tell what response would resolve the letter. The improved version states the other side's contention in controlled indirect speech, answers it with the specific clause that governs the point, states the consequence that follows, and closes with a dated request tied to a concrete document. Every figure and date comes from the facts of the matter as instructed; nothing is invented or left as a placeholder.

## Example 2: submission paragraph linking fact, authority and conclusion

### Scenario

Counsel for Rosewood Timber Supplies Pty Ltd files written submissions resisting a claim by Denholm Builders Pty Ltd for damages arising from a late delivery of framing timber under a supply contract dated 4 February 2026. Denholm alleges that its site supervisor and Rosewood's sales manager agreed by phone on 18 March 2026 to extend the delivery date from 1 April 2026 to 15 April 2026, and that Rosewood is bound by that agreement. Clause 9.2 of the supply contract provides that any variation to a delivery date must be given in a notice signed by an authorised representative of both parties. No such notice was given.

### Poor version

It is submitted that the contract is clear on this point and that no variation occurred. The alleged phone call does not change the contract. The delivery date therefore remained 1 April 2026, and the applicant is liable for late delivery.

### Improved version

It is submitted that the delivery date under the supply contract remained 1 April 2026. Clause 9.2 requires any variation to a delivery date to be given in a notice signed by an authorised representative of each party. The applicant does not allege that any such notice was given; it relies solely on an unrecorded telephone conversation between a site supervisor and a sales manager, neither described as an authorised representative for the purpose of clause 9.2. In the fictional decision of Carrow v Whitlam Estates Pty Ltd, a case name invented purely for this illustration, a clause in materially the same terms was held to exclude an informal variation made by personnel without authority to bind the party, even where the other party acted on the conversation in the interim. Applying that reasoning, the phone call of 18 March 2026 did not vary the delivery date. The date therefore remained 1 April 2026, and the respondent's delivery on 22 April 2026 was late under the contract as written.

### Why the improved version works

Written submissions are persuasive, but the persuasion has to be built from fact, rule and application rather than from assertion. The poor version states a conclusion twice without ever connecting the clause, the alleged conversation and the authority that resolves the tension between them, so a reader cannot check the reasoning. The improved version follows the proposition, fact, authority, application and conclusion structure: it states what clause 9.2 requires, identifies exactly what the applicant does and does not allege, applies the fictional authority to the specific gap in the applicant's case, and reaches a conclusion that follows from the steps before it. The authority is expressly labelled as fictional so it is never mistaken for a real citation.

## Example 3: balanced advice paragraph stating risk and counterargument

### Scenario

A solicitor advises Harrow Funds Management Pty Ltd, the landlord from example 1, on the prospects of enforcing the reviewed rent of $86,400 per annum against Bellcourt Cafe Pty Ltd. The file shows that after the disputed phone call of 12 January 2026, Harrow Funds continued to invoice Bellcourt at the old rent of $81,600 per annum for six further invoices, from January to June 2026, before the property manager identified the error in July 2026.

### Poor version

Our client's position is strong. The lease requires a written waiver, and none exists, so the review will apply. There is no real risk to this position and we recommend proceeding to invoice the increased amount immediately.

### Improved version

The lease favours our client's position on its face: clause 22.3 requires a signed written waiver, and none exists. There is, however, a real risk that Bellcourt could raise an equitable estoppel argument, because our client continued to invoice at the old rent of $81,600 for six months after the disputed phone call, from January to June 2026, before the error was identified. That conduct is capable of being characterised as our client acting consistently with the waiver Bellcourt alleges, which could support a finding that our client is estopped from enforcing the review for that period even without a signed document. On balance, we consider our client is more likely than not to succeed in enforcing the review from July 2026 onward, but the estoppel argument creates a genuine risk in relation to the January to June period, and we recommend a without prejudice conversation with Bellcourt before formal steps are taken.

### Why the improved version works

An advice is an analytical document for a reader who needs a candid answer rather than a persuasive one, so a one sided assessment fails the document's purpose even where the client would prefer to hear only the favourable view. The poor version states a conclusion without engaging with the client's own conduct, which is the fact most likely to be raised against the client in any dispute. The improved version states the favourable contractual position, then states the specific risk created by the client's own six months of invoicing at the old rate, explains why that conduct matters in estoppel terms, and reaches a conclusion that distinguishes between the period at risk and the period that is safer. That is what makes the advice useful: it tells the client where the real risk sits, as well as where the client is strong.

## Example 4: neutral file note recording instructions without advocacy

### Scenario

A solicitor at the firm acting for Harrow Funds Management Pty Ltd records a telephone call with the client's director, Priya Nair, following the advice in example 3.

### Poor version

Spoke to the client today. Explained our client is clearly in the right on this lease dispute and that Bellcourt has no real answer to the rent review. Client agreed with our strong view and instructed us to press ahead and demand payment at once.

### Improved version

12 July 2026, 3.10pm, telephone call. Participants: the writer and Priya Nair, director of Harrow Funds Management Pty Ltd. Subject: prospects of enforcing the CPI rent review against Bellcourt Cafe Pty Ltd. Information given: advised that the lease favours the client's position from July 2026 onward, but that the client's own invoicing at the old rent for six months, from January to June 2026, creates a real risk of an estoppel argument for that period. Information received: Ms Nair confirmed the property manager's invoicing error was not deliberate and that no separate conversation about waiving the review occurred beyond the one already disclosed. Instructions received: proceed with a without prejudice letter proposing that the reviewed rent apply from July 2026 without seeking to recover the shortfall for the January to June period. Next action: the writer to prepare the without prejudice letter by 17 July 2026.

### Why the improved version works

A file note is an evidentiary record, and its value depends on a later reader being able to trust it as a neutral account of what was said and instructed rather than as advocacy dressed up as a record. The poor version substitutes the writer's own view of the merits for a record of what actually happened on the call, and it invents a stronger instruction than the client is shown as having given. The improved version follows the neutral structure: date and method, participants, subject, information given, information received, instructions received, and next action with a responsible person and a date. It records the compromise the client actually instructed instead of the more aggressive position the poor version assumed the client wanted.
